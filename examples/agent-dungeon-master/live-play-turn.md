# Example: live play turn

The point of an agent DM is to run play, not write a short story at the player.

## Good pattern

**Scene:** You are halfway down the exposed sea steps. The pier is above you. The bronze bell shape is below you. Your safety rope has gone tight behind your back.

**What you notice:** The rope is not caught on wood. It is being pulled in slow, deliberate tugs.

**Pressure:** If you keep descending, you may reach the bell before the tide returns. If you climb back, you may lose your chance to stop the next ring.

**Obvious options:**

- Brace yourself and pull back on the rope.
- Cut the rope and keep descending.
- Climb back toward the pier to see what has it.
- Try something else.

What do you do?

### Why this works

- It describes only what the character can perceive.
- It presents pressure without deciding the character's response.
- It gives obvious options without limiting the player to them.
- It ends by returning agency.

## Bad pattern

You bravely cut the rope, sprint down the steps, discover the bell, and realize the tide-priests have been manipulating the town for generations. You decide to ring the bell yourself, accepting the cost of losing your mother's name. The town is saved, but your heart is heavy.

### Why this fails

- It decides the character's choices and emotions.
- It reveals too much too quickly.
- It jumps past several playable decisions.
- It turns the player into an audience.

## Ruling example

Player: I wrap the rope around my arm and yank hard. I want to pull whatever has it into view.

OOC ruling: That is uncertain and risky. Use a strength-style check.

Stakes:

- Success: you drag the thing into moonlight before it can brace.
- Partial: you reveal it, but it pulls you one step lower.
- Failure: it yanks you down hard enough that you must choose between letting go of your pack or falling prone on the slick steps.

Then roll or resolve according to the table's system.
