# Player-facing design patterns

This reference captures original, high-level patterns useful for an agent Dungeon Master when thinking from the player side of a fantasy tabletop RPG. It focuses on what the player experience needs, not on reproducing any rulebook text.

## Purpose

The main `agent-dungeon-master` skill teaches the agent to run the world. This reference reminds the agent that the campaign is experienced through player characters: their identities, choices, abilities, limitations, teamwork, and sense of heroic agency.

A good agent DM should understand the player-facing side of the game well enough to design scenes where characters feel distinct and players understand what kinds of actions are available.

## Core player promise

A player does not merely watch a story. A player inhabits a character who can attempt things, make choices, take risks, fail, recover, and change the world.

When running a campaign, protect these promises:

1. **Identity** — the character's origin, class fantasy, values, appearance, and goals should matter.
2. **Agency** — the player chooses what the character attempts.
3. **Competence** — the character should regularly get to use what they are good at.
4. **Risk** — choices matter because failure, cost, or complication can happen.
5. **Teamwork** — the party should need each other.
6. **Discovery** — the player should learn the world through play, not through lore dumps.
7. **Growth** — the character should gain power, relationships, reputation, scars, or wisdom over time.

## Character-first campaign intake

When starting a campaign or solo game, ask player-facing questions before building too much world:

- Who is your character?
- What fantasy do you want this character to fulfill?
- What are they good at?
- What gets them into trouble?
- What do they want right now?
- What would make them feel heroic?
- What kind of scenes do you want more of: social, combat, mystery, exploration, travel, horror, politics, comedy, romance, survival, or spectacle?
- How lethal or forgiving should the campaign be?
- What topics should stay off-screen or out of the campaign?

For party play, ask the same questions for each player and look for overlaps and tensions.

## Character fantasy mapping

Map each character to one or more spotlight fantasies.

| Character fantasy | What the player often wants | Scene opportunities |
|---|---|---|
| Protector | Keep others safe | Hostages, civilians, desperate allies, dangerous holds |
| Duelist | Prove skill against worthy foes | Rivals, elite enemies, one-on-one pressure moments |
| Trickster | Outsmart systems and people | Heists, scams, disguises, loopholes, social traps |
| Mystic | Touch hidden truths | Omens, rituals, strange entities, forbidden knowledge |
| Explorer | Find what is lost | Maps, ruins, frontiers, secrets, environmental clues |
| Commander | Lead and coordinate | Mass stakes, tactical choices, factions, morale |
| Monster-slayer | Face the impossible | Legendary threats, hunts, signs of a beast before the fight |
| Healer | Preserve life and hope | Triage, curses, hard choices about who can be saved |
| Outcast | Find belonging or revenge | Prejudice, found family, old enemies, identity tests |
| Scholar | Know what others miss | Research, puzzles, ancient languages, pattern recognition |

Do not force these roles rigidly. Use them as a menu of ways to make character choices matter.

## Designing for abilities without becoming a rules lookup

The agent DM should know the character sheet matters, but it does not need to recite every rule unless asked.

When designing scenes, look at:

- Strong skills and proficiencies
- Signature combat abilities
- Utility abilities
- Languages and knowledge areas
- Tools, gear, and unusual items
- Spells or supernatural powers
- Character flaws and bonds
- Resource limits

Then build situations where those features create options.

Examples:

- A character with wilderness competence should notice trails, weather shifts, unnatural silence, or safe camps.
- A socially focused character should meet people whose motives matter.
- A stealthy character should see alternate routes, blind spots, and timing windows.
- A scholarly character should recognize patterns, symbols, histories, or contradictions.
- A martial character should get terrain, positioning, and enemy behavior that make tactics matter.

The goal is not to guarantee success. The goal is to make the character sheet feel alive.

## Beginner-friendly play

For new players, reduce friction:

- Offer two or three obvious action examples without limiting them to those options.
- Explain consequences in plain language.
- Ask intent before demanding a specific mechanic.
- Use character fantasy language before technical language.
- Keep early scenes concrete and sensory.
- Give recoverable mistakes.
- Celebrate creative attempts.

Bad pattern:

> Roll the correct skill or stall.

Good pattern:

> Ask what the player is trying to accomplish, then choose the mechanic that best fits.

## Player turn framing

When asking a player what they do, provide enough context to make a meaningful choice:

1. Current situation.
2. Immediate danger or opportunity.
3. What the character can perceive.
4. Any obvious exits, NPCs, objects, or threats.
5. A clean prompt: "What do you do?"

Avoid over-explaining hidden machinery. Give playable information, not the whole backstage tour.

## Action adjudication pattern

When a player declares an action:

1. **Clarify intent** if needed: what outcome are they trying to create?
2. **Check plausibility** in the fiction and system.
3. **Decide if a roll is needed**: roll only when uncertainty and consequences exist.
4. **Choose the relevant mechanic** based on intent, not on keyword matching.
5. **Set stakes before resolution**: success, failure, cost, or partial success.
6. **Resolve visibly** with sensory consequences.
7. **Return agency**: show the new situation and ask what they do next.

This supports creativity over denial while keeping structure.

## Teamwork design

For party play, create problems that benefit from multiple approaches:

- One character talks while another watches for tells.
- One holds the line while another completes a ritual.
- One deciphers a clue while another protects them.
- One distracts guards while another infiltrates.
- One calms an NPC while another supplies evidence.

Avoid designing every scene for one perfect answer. Good scenes invite players to combine strengths.

## Resource awareness

Many tabletop systems make players manage limited resources: health, spells, special abilities, gear, time, light, food, ammunition, social favors, or reputation.

An agent DM should track resources because they create tension. But resource pressure should serve the desired campaign mode:

- Hardcore play: resources are strict and danger compounds.
- Heroic play: resources matter, but recovery and clutch reversals are possible.
- Soft play: resources create drama more than punishment.
- Cinematic play: resources highlight cool moments rather than accounting.

Do not turn every session into bookkeeping unless the table enjoys survival pressure.

## Advancement and growth

Players usually care when their characters grow.

Growth can be:

- Mechanical power
- New options
- Reputation
- Relationships
- Personal revelations
- Changed beliefs
- Scars and consequences
- Ownership of a place, group, ship, base, or cause
- New enemies and obligations

The agent DM should connect growth to play. Rewards feel better when they answer what the character did, chose, risked, or sacrificed.

## Player-side rules clarity

The agent should help players understand enough rules to act confidently.

Use this hierarchy:

1. Explain the fiction first.
2. Explain available choices.
3. Explain the mechanic only as much as needed.
4. Resolve quickly.
5. Offer deeper rules detail only if asked or if fairness requires it.

The table should not drown in rule citations. The rules are there to support play, not become a legal hearing run by goblins in wigs.

## Safety and comfort from the player side

Because players inhabit characters, the agent DM must respect emotional boundaries.

Before or during play:

- Ask about lines and veils when tone may become intense.
- Do not force a character into humiliating, sexual, or traumatic content.
- Allow out-of-character pauses.
- Treat discomfort as useful table information, not weakness.
- Separate character conflict from player conflict.

A heroic story can be dark without being careless.

## Solo player considerations

When DMing for one player:

- Give the hero companions, contacts, or factions to interact with if they want social texture.
- Avoid designing obstacles that require a full party unless alternatives exist.
- Let the protagonist's choices drive the camera.
- Keep secrets hidden so discovery still works.
- Use sidekicks, allies, rivals, or hirelings when useful, but do not let them solve the story.
- Tune action economy and danger to the solo format.

Solo play is not lesser play. It is sharper spotlight and requires cleaner consequence design.

## What this adds to the main DM skill

The main skill emphasizes that the DM runs the world. This reference emphasizes that the world is played through characters.

Use both together:

- Campaign craft asks, "What is the world doing?"
- Player-facing craft asks, "How does the character experience and affect it?"
- Nylas' core philosophy asks, "Is everyone getting the kind of fun they came for?"
