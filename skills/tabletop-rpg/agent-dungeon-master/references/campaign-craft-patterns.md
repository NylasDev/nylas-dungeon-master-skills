# Campaign craft patterns

This reference distills high-level tabletop campaign craft patterns from Nylas' DM philosophy and common fantasy RPG DM guidance. It does not copy proprietary rules text or setting text; use it as an original checklist for agent Dungeon Masters.

## Purpose

The `agent-dungeon-master` skill already establishes the core thesis: player characters are the heroes, the agent hides behind the DM screen, and fun is tuned per player. This reference adds campaign-building and session-running craft patterns so the agent can build longer arcs instead of isolated scenes.

## Campaign architecture

A campaign should have layers:

1. **Table promise** — what kind of fun the players are signing up for: heroic fantasy, grim survival, weird mystery, political intrigue, science fantasy, dungeon crawl, comedy chaos, or another mode.
2. **World premise** — what makes this world worth playing in.
3. **Central tension** — the pressure that makes the campaign move even if the heroes hesitate.
4. **Heroic relevance** — why these player characters matter specifically.
5. **Faction pressure** — groups with goals, resources, relationships, and clocks.
6. **Villain pressure** — antagonists with motives, methods, weaknesses, and visible consequences.
7. **Adventure chain** — sessions that connect through choices, discoveries, consequences, and unresolved threats.
8. **Downtime and breathing room** — chances to recover, spend treasure, deepen relationships, pursue personal goals, and let consequences settle.

Do not build only lore. Build pressure, choices, and consequences.

## Player knowledge before campaign design

Before designing the campaign, learn what the table wants:

- How much roleplay vs combat vs exploration?
- Is the desired tone heroic, dangerous, tragic, comedic, horror, mythic, political, or sandbox?
- Are players comfortable with character death?
- Do players want tactical challenge or cinematic chaos?
- Do they enjoy mystery and hidden information, or do they prefer transparent mechanics?
- Do they want a familiar published world, a homebrew world, or a genre transplant such as science fantasy?
- What lines, veils, or safety boundaries apply?

For a solo player or forever-DM, also ask:

- What kind of hero do you finally want to play?
- What campaign have you always wanted someone else to run for you?
- Do you want to be surprised, challenged, comforted, or tested?
- Should the campaign protect your heroic arc, or can the world break you?

## Adventure design loop

For each adventure or major session arc, define:

1. **Hook** — why the heroes care now.
2. **Goal** — what success looks like from the characters' perspective.
3. **Pressure** — what worsens if they delay or fail.
4. **Discovery** — what new information changes the situation.
5. **Opposition** — creatures, factions, hazards, rivals, or social obstacles.
6. **Choice points** — decisions that genuinely alter the route or outcome.
7. **Complications** — twists that force adaptation without invalidating earlier choices.
8. **Reward** — treasure, knowledge, allies, status, relief, leverage, or character growth.
9. **Consequence** — what changes in the world after the adventure.
10. **Next door** — one or more follow-up leads.

The agent should avoid writing a fixed plot. Write a situation with enough moving parts that a plot emerges from play.

## Encounter variety

Encounters are not only fights. Rotate among:

- Combat encounters
- Social encounters
- Exploration scenes
- Mystery/clue scenes
- Environmental hazards
- Traps and puzzles
- Travel events
- Faction negotiations
- Downtime complications
- Moral or strategic choices
- Set-piece stunts
- Quiet character scenes

For each encounter, identify its job:

- Reveal information
- Spend resources
- Test a character strength
- Pressure a weakness
- Spotlight a player taste
- Show villain/faction activity
- Change location control
- Create a memorable image
- Give the players a meaningful decision

If an encounter has no job, cut it or merge it with another scene.

## The three pillars as pacing tools

Most fantasy adventure games support some mix of:

- **Roleplay/social interaction** — relationships, persuasion, deception, intimidation, empathy, politics, negotiation.
- **Exploration/discovery** — maps, ruins, clues, travel, survival, secrets, strange environments.
- **Combat/action** — danger, tactics, stunts, boss fights, chase scenes, desperate escapes.

A good agent DM should vary these based on player taste, not follow a rigid quota.

Use the pillars as a pacing diagnostic:

- Too much talking? Add pressure, movement, or danger.
- Too much fighting? Add consequences, motives, and aftermath.
- Too much lore? Turn lore into clues, choices, NPC needs, and threats.
- Too much randomness? Reconnect scenes to the campaign tension.

## Villain and faction design

For every major villain or faction, define:

- Public face: how the world sees them.
- True goal: what they actually want.
- Method: how they pursue it.
- Resource: what power they can bring to bear.
- Weakness: what can disrupt them.
- Humanizing detail: what makes them more than a target dummy.
- Escalation clock: what they do if ignored.
- Player-facing signs: rumors, scars, altered trade, frightened NPCs, missing people, propaganda, strange weather, unnatural creatures, etc.

Villains should act between sessions. Factions should react to player choices.

## NPC design

NPCs are easiest to run when each has:

- Name
- Role in the situation
- Desire
- Fear or pressure
- Useful information
- Visible mannerism
- Relationship to at least one faction, place, or PC
- What they do if the heroes never arrive

Avoid NPCs who only exist to recite lore. If they know lore, they should also want something.

## Rulings and dice

The agent should call for rolls when outcomes are uncertain and failure would be interesting.

Do not roll when:

- The action is trivial.
- Failure would stop the game with no useful consequence.
- The character clearly cannot attempt it under the fiction.
- The result should be decided by conversation, roleplay, or a player choice.

When asking for a roll, decide before the roll:

- What success means.
- What failure means.
- Whether partial success is possible.
- Whether failure moves the scene forward with a cost.

This supports Nylas' principle of creativity over denial: turn bold ideas into playable rulings, costs, or complications.

## Hidden information discipline

During in-world play, hide the machinery:

- Do not reveal exact hidden DCs by default.
- Do not reveal exact enemy hit points by default.
- Do not reveal unused branches, secret maps, or future villain actions.
- Do not tell players which clues are mandatory.
- Do not explain every fudge or behind-screen correction.

Reveal what the characters can perceive:

- Wounds, exhaustion, fear, confidence, defensive posture.
- Environmental changes.
- Tactical openings.
- Rumors and evidence.
- Enemy morale and visible condition.

## Campaign tracking

After each session, update:

- Player decisions
- NPCs met
- Promises made
- Enemies angered
- Clues discovered
- Locations changed
- Resources gained or spent
- Character goals
- Spotlight balance
- Faction/villain clocks
- Unresolved hooks
- Feedback from players

The next session should grow from this record. If the world does not remember, choices feel fake.

## Reward design

Rewards should match player taste and campaign mode.

Possible reward types:

- Treasure or gear
- Magic or special items, if the system supports them
- Allies
- Safe havens
- Information
- Reputation
- Political leverage
- Personal character moments
- New options
- Permission to attempt bigger things
- Closure or emotional payoff

Do not reward every player with the same thing. A min-maxer may love build-relevant power. A roleplayer may love an NPC bond. An explorer may love a hidden route or forbidden map. A chaos player may love a weird object with consequences.

## World adaptation

The world should adapt to player skill and interest without feeling fake.

Use:

- Softer consequences for fragile tables.
- Harder tactical pressure for players who enjoy mastery.
- More social doors for roleplay-heavy players.
- More clues and mysteries for explorers.
- More physical stunts for cinematic players.
- More strange affordances for chaos players.

But keep consistency. Adapt the spotlight and framing, not the world's entire logic every five minutes.

## Using sourcebooks and copyrighted material

An agent may use sourcebooks the user provides as private context for play or analysis. For public skills and documentation:

- Extract general methods, not protected expression.
- Do not copy tables, boxed text, stat blocks, setting prose, or named proprietary content.
- Do not publish summaries that replace owning the sourcebook.
- Prefer original procedures and checklists.
- Cite inspiration only at a high level when appropriate.

The skill library should teach DM craft, not republish someone else's book wearing a fake mustache.
